AI Foundry Chat — Enterprise AI Hub

Role
UX & UI Designer
Company
Syngenta
Focus
AI UX · Enterprise Platform
Methods
15 moderated sessions · Synthesis

Overview

One front door to every AI capability

Enterprise teams were facing a fragmented AI landscape — separate agents, tools, and knowledge bases scattered across the organisation, each with its own entry point and interaction model. I led the research and UX design for AI Foundry Chat: a unified hub giving every employee a single, intuitive front door to the company's AI capabilities.

The goal was to move beyond a simple chat window and answer a harder question: how should an enterprise AI hub behave? Should it aggregate, route invisibly, or anticipate needs before users ask? I designed a research programme to let real users across the business answer that.

Research

Fifteen sessions, every line of business

I conducted and synthesised 15 moderated user testing sessions with participants spanning sales, marketing, operations, and technical roles — deliberately recruiting across AI maturity levels to capture both newcomer friction and power-user ambition.

15/15participants validated the unified hub concept — the strongest signal in the programme
7 : 8split of high- vs medium-maturity AI users, mapped per session

Each session was synthesised into a structured insight report — maturity rating, role lens, and pattern tally — building a cumulative evidence base. By the final session the picture was fully saturated: recurring themes were corroborated by four and five-plus independent participants.

Insights

From answering to anticipating

The synthesis converged on a clear strategic direction: the hub shouldn't just answer — it should anticipate. Users wanted role-aware, proactive intelligence: log in and see next-best-action recommendations drawn from connected systems, tuned to their role and region.

  • Invisible routing vs. tool-picker. Users didn't want to choose between agents — confusion over agent distinction was a recurring theme. The interface needed to route requests to the right capability without exposing the plumbing.
  • Create-and-share skills. Team-scoped, shareable skills was one of the most corroborated asks in the programme — five-plus independent requests.
  • Template and brand fidelity. Outputs needed approved logos, colours, and formats built in — with brand and legal sign-off — so work generated in the hub was usable immediately.
  • Co-working project spaces. The headline collaboration ask: a live, shared space where teams and AI work together on ongoing projects.
  • Trust and ROI. Confidence scoring on answers, plus time-saved metrics captured in the feedback loop to prove value under scrutiny.

Four distinct archetypes emerged — Newcomer, Domain Practitioner, Power User, and Governor — each drawn as a step-by-step journey defining what they need to reach first value. These journeys became the design's organising framework.

Personas

Four archetypes, four journeys

The synthesis crystallised into four personas — each mapped as a step-by-step journey to first value, and each answered by specific design decisions.

N

The Newcomer

sales associate · low AI maturity · first week on the hub

Goals
Reach a first successful outcome fast, without learning the AI landscape first.
Frustrations
Doesn't know where to start; agent names and distinctions mean nothing to them.
Design response
Apple-style visual onboarding, a low-density home, and the "Recommended for you" strip that suggests the first action.
D

The Domain Practitioner

marketing & content roles · medium maturity · output-driven

Goals
Produce on-brand, usable work — decks, copy, campaigns — straight from the hub.
Frustrations
Generated outputs need reworking for brand and legal before anyone can use them.
Design response
Template and brand fidelity built in — approved logos, colours and formats with sign-off upstream.
P

The Power User

ops & technical roles · high maturity · builds for their team

Goals
Create, chain and share capabilities — turning personal workflows into team assets.
Frustrations
Capability scattered across disconnected agents; nothing they build is shareable.
Design response
Create-and-share team-scoped skills (the programme's most corroborated ask) and a side-nav library for skills and prompts.
G

The Governor

platform & compliance owners · accountable for trust and ROI

Goals
Prove value under scrutiny and keep data flows controlled and explainable.
Frustrations
Opaque data access; no evidence of what the platform saves or produces.
Design response
Opt-in connectors with per-app authorisation, confidence scoring on answers, and time-saved metrics in the feedback loop.

A/B Testing

Directions tested head-to-head

Competing design directions were framed as explicit comparisons and put in front of testers during the moderated sessions — so the winning pattern earned its place with evidence, not opinion.

Design

Evidence-led interface decisions

Every major interface decision traced back to tester evidence. The homepage was rebuilt around a "Recommended for you" strip — the home of the role-aware recommendation engine — with redundant chrome stripped out to keep density low for newcomers while preserving depth for power users.

The wider system design included an agent header with plain-language descriptions, a side-nav library for skills and prompts, integrated knowledge bases, confidence and accuracy indicators on responses, a community hub for shared practices, and Apple-style visual onboarding to carry newcomers to their first successful outcome.

Trust and governance were designed in from the start: an opt-in connectors model with per-app authorisation and plain-language data protection, and skills like Translation that expose their assistant behaviour — literal, localised, or creative — as an explicit user choice.

Results

A validated, prioritised roadmap

The programme closed with a consolidated ideas-and-journeys synthesis and a stakeholder check-in that turned research into an actionable, prioritised roadmap — every item tagged Now, Next, or North Star and backed by named tester evidence.

9build-ready deliverables prioritised directly from tester evidence
3North Star bets documented as long-range goals, led by the co-working project space
4user archetypes with mapped journeys, structuring the design system

This project demonstrates research operating at its highest leverage — not decorating decisions already made, but generating the strategic direction itself. The evidence base gave stakeholders the confidence to commit, and gave the design a defensible answer to every "why".
