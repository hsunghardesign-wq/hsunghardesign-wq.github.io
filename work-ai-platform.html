Enterprise AI

Designing an enterprise AI knowledge platform.

UX/UI for an internal AI-powered knowledge hub — from the SSO entry experience to the system states nobody usually designs for. The journey below shows how research, personas and A/B thinking shaped every decision, including turning a maintenance screen into a release-marketing moment.

Role

Product & UX/UI Designer

Sector

Enterprise AI / Knowledge Management

Focus

Portal UX, System States, Design Tokens

Tools

Figma, Design Tokens, HTML/CSS

Research

Understanding how people meet an AI platform — and what happens when it's down.

Before designing, I mapped how users encounter the platform: the entry flow, the moments of friction, and the overlooked states — downtime, upgrades, first visits — where trust is won or lost.

Entry-flow audit

Reviewed the SSO login journey end to end. Single-button SSO entry with "remember me" reduced friction, but the surrounding page communicated nothing about the product's value.

System-state inventory

Catalogued every state a user could hit — loading, empty, error, scheduled maintenance. The 6–8 hour upgrade window was the highest-visibility state with the lowest design investment.

Stakeholder feedback loops

Design feedback highlighted an opportunity: rather than apologising for downtime, use the screen to build anticipation for the release — a marketing moment, not an outage notice.

Token & system alignment

Grounded every screen in the platform's design tokens — brand violet, spacing and type scales — so new states shipped consistent with the product, not bolted on.

Personas

Who we designed for.

Two personas anchored the decisions — the everyday knowledge worker, and the stakeholder who answers for the platform.

A

Amira, 34 — The Knowledge Worker

Uses the hub daily · Time-poor · Trusts what's consistent

Goals
Sign in fast with SSO, find answers quickly, and know exactly when the platform will be back if it's ever unavailable.
Frustrations
Vague outage messages with no timeline; interfaces that change without explanation after upgrades.
Design response
A clear maintenance screen with the expected window stated up front, "what's changing" explained, and a progress indicator — so downtime informs instead of frustrates.
R

Raj, 45 — The Platform Stakeholder

Owns adoption metrics · Communicates releases · Answers to leadership

Goals
Turn each release into visible momentum; keep users positive about the platform even during downtime.
Frustrations
Apologetic outage notices that dent confidence; missed chances to market what the team shipped.
Design response
The downtime screen doubles as release marketing — "We're upgrading your knowledge experience" with feature teasers, making the outage itself an announcement.
The Journey

Scenarios that shaped the design.

Scenario 01 — “Locked out, but informed”

Amira opens the hub during the upgrade window. Instead of an error, she sees a branded page: what's being upgraded, why it matters, how long it will take, and a progress bar. She closes the tab knowing exactly when to come back.

Visit during downtime→Branded maintenance page→"What's changing?"→Time expectation set→Returns confident

Scenario 02 — “The release as a moment”

Raj shares the maintenance screen in the release comms. Because it teases what's coming rather than apologising, the upgrade lands as news — and post-release traffic spikes as users return to see what changed.

Release scheduled→Teaser screen live→Comms reuse the design→Anticipation built→Users return on day one

Scenario 03 — “First sign-in”

A new team member hits the portal, sees the product identity clearly, and signs in with one SSO tap — no forms, no friction, "remember me" on by default for their next visit.

Land on portal→Clear product identity→One-tap SSO→Remembered next visit
A/B Testing & Iteration

Decisions tested, not guessed.

Key design directions were framed as experiments — compared, reviewed with stakeholders, and iterated before shipping.

ExperimentHypothesisVariant AVariant BOutcome
Downtime treatment A full-page takeover communicates better than a small modal Modal over the login screen Full-page branded maintenance experience B chosen — modal felt like an afterthought
Downtime tone Informational clarity beats mystery for enterprise users Dark countdown teaser ("something is coming") Light informational layout with "What's changing?" B chosen after stakeholder review
Time expectation Stating the window up front reduces support queries "We'll be back soon" Explicit "approximately 6–8 hours" in bold B shipped — clarity over vagueness
Progress feedback A visible progress bar reduces perceived wait Static text only Progress bar + stat cards (window / new / more) B shipped — the page feels alive

Outcomes reflect design review decisions; quantitative results to be added post-release.

Outcome

Every state now speaks the brand's language.

The maintenance experience

A full-page, on-brand upgrade screen — scheduled-maintenance badge, "We're upgrading your knowledge experience" headline, explicit 6–8 hour window, "What's changing?" card with progress, and stat cards — designed in Figma on the platform's tokens and prototyped in working HTML/CSS.
