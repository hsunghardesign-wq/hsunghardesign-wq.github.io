Overview

From documents to a working assistant

The AI Knowledge Hub turns knowledge into capability: upload or connect any set of sources — SharePoint sites, Confluence spaces, Amazon S3 buckets, or direct file uploads — and the platform builds a dedicated AI assistant grounded in exactly that content. Teams can spin up as many assistants as they have bodies of knowledge: an HR policy bot, a product spec bot, a compliance bot — each scoped to its own sources.

I designed the end-to-end experience: the creation wizard, source management, quality evaluation, sharing, and the notification loop that brings collaborators in.

Personas

Owners, consumers, governors

The archetypes from the AI Foundry research programme carried directly into the Knowledge Hub — reframed around what people do with knowledge: those who own it, those who consume it, and those accountable for it.

Creation flow

A wizard anyone can finish

Creating an assistant is a guided four-step flow, with a persistent assistant guide explaining each decision in plain language:

  • Step 1 — Describe the assistant. Name it and define its purpose, so everyone who later finds it knows exactly what it is for.
  • Step 2 — Connect the sources. Choose where the AI pulls its information — SharePoint, Confluence, Amazon S3, or direct file upload — and define the kinds of questions the assistant will be asked to answer. Multiple connectors can feed one knowledge base, each managing its own authentication.
  • Step 3 — Set management and access. Decide who has access to the project — and with it, the authority to ask the assistant questions. Access and governance are one decision, not an afterthought.
  • Step 4 — Review. A final check that the description, sources, and access are all correct before the assistant goes live.

Quality

Trust you can measure

An assistant is only as good as its sources — so every knowledge base gets a quality score measured against a target benchmark. The evaluation page pinpoints exactly which assets are failing and why, flags high-priority faults, and offers one-click re-evaluate and re-upload actions to close the loop.

This reframes knowledge management from a filing exercise into an accountability loop: owners can see their assistant's health at a glance, and users can trust that a "Ready" assistant meets the benchmark.

AI Readiness

What makes a document ready for AI

The quality score is built on a deeper piece of research: understanding why documents fail when they meet an AI. A document written for humans assumes a reader who sees the whole file, follows the links, and knows the context. Retrieval-augmented AI breaks every one of those assumptions — it retrieves chunks, in isolation, with no author on hand to explain.

  • Chunked retrieval changes the rules. Only a fragment of a document may be retrieved for any given question — so every section has to stand alone. Phrases like "as defined in section X" or "see linked policy" leave the AI holding a reference it cannot follow.
  • Metadata lives in two places. Some readiness signals belong in the document itself (owner, dates, version); others — tags, audience, region, language — are maintained in the content register. The criteria distinguish what to look for in each, so documents aren't penalised for register-managed fields.
  • Readiness is measurable. Turning these failure modes into scored criteria is what lets the platform tell an owner exactly which asset is dragging the assistant down, and why.

Every document is evaluated against the five official KQS dimensions:

kqs_01

Retrievable

  • Clear, search-optimised title
  • Useful, consistent tags and metadata where visible
  • No duplicates or near-duplicate content
  • Easy to locate via search in its repository
  • Register-managed fields (tags, audience, region, language) assessed in the content register, not the document

kqs_02

Clear

  • Plain, unambiguous language
  • Logical headings and short, readable paragraphs — no walls of text
  • Easy for both humans and LLMs to parse
  • Images described via alt-text or captions (noted, not penalised, when absent)
  • Self-contained language — understandable without following a cross-reference or link

kqs_03

Relevant

  • Directly addresses the business need and real user questions
  • Actionable and problem-solving — the "how to" or "why" is explicit
  • Includes steps, guidance or decisions, not just abstract description
  • Links to related material — but critical information is never hidden entirely behind a link

kqs_04

Accurate

  • Factually correct and precise content
  • Ownership, approval or source visible — or inferable
  • No contradictions or outdated information
  • Version control and explicit dates where relevant

kqs_05

Time-bound

  • Owner, last-updated date and version embedded in the document itself
  • Indications of review cadence and a feedback channel
  • Clearly positioned as current, draft, or deprecated
  • Suitable for continuous improvement over time
Why it matters: an assistant can only be as trustworthy as its worst frequently-retrieved chunk. Scoring documents on these five dimensions before they reach the AI turns "garbage in, garbage out" into a governed, improvable pipeline — and gives every knowledge owner a concrete to-do list instead of a vague quality warning.

RAG Readiness

Self-contained, or silently misleading

A dedicated check sits within the Clear and Relevant dimensions: the RAG self-containedness assessment. Corporate documents — policies, governance frameworks, procedural guides — lean heavily on cross-references and hyperlinks. Fine for a human navigating a document library; a real risk for a RAG system, which retrieves a chunk in isolation. The cross-referenced content simply isn't there, and the AI can produce incomplete or misleading answers without ever knowing it.

Every document is assessed on four explicit checks:

  • Cross-reference density. How often does the document defer to external documents, sections or systems for essential information? "See also", "refer to", "as defined in", "per policy X" — counted or estimated, and flagged when high.
  • Definition dependency. Are key terms, thresholds, roles or rules defined only elsewhere? If this document partially answers a question, will the AI be unable to complete the answer because the definition lives in another file?
  • Procedural completeness. For any process described, are the steps complete here — or do critical steps redirect to another document entirely?
  • Link-rot risk. Live URLs and references to documents that may move, be renamed, or disappear. Static references are more durable than links, but both fail if the target isn't also in the RAG system.

Findings feed directly into scoring — a graduated penalty ladder rather than a binary pass/fail:

FindingImpact on score
Largely self-contained; only optional supplementary referencesNo penalty
Cross-references for context, but enough inline information to stay usefulMinor — −0.5 on the affected dimension
Critical information routinely deferred with no inline summarySignificant — −1 to −2 on Clear and/or Relevant
Essentially an index pointing at other documents, no standalone contentClear and Relevant scored 1–2

Metadata Model

Two homes for metadata

A key insight from the knowledge-management workstream: not all metadata belongs inside the document. A central content register captures and manages most fields — so the evaluation deliberately distinguishes what lives where, and never penalises a document for register-managed fields.

category_a — content register

Managed centrally, not in the document

  • Title, link, description / abstract
  • Business unit, region, country, site
  • Tags / keywords, language, audience
  • File format and content source
  • Access control, confidentiality level, regulatory tags
  • Linked workflows, referenced documents, curation cadence

category_b — document-embedded

Must travel with the content

  • Author — who wrote it
  • Owner — the accountable business owner
  • Version — current version number
  • Creation date — when it was first created
  • Modification / last-reviewed date — when it was last updated

Why the split matters: when the RAG system retrieves a chunk, the register isn't there to vouch for it. The five Category B fields are the provenance and trust signals that travel with the content at inference time — so they must be visible in the document itself (header, footer, cover page or metadata table), and they gate the Accurate and Time-bound scores:

Category B fields presentImpact on score
All five, clearly visibleNo penalty
3–4 presentMinor gap — −0.5, missing fields noted
1–2 presentSignificant gap — −1, flagged as High recommendation
None presentMajor issue — −1 to −2, flagged as Critical

Every evaluation output includes a provenance table, so owners see at a glance which trust signals their document carries:

FieldPresent in document?Notes
AuthorYesFound in footer
OwnerPartialTeam named, no accountable individual
VersionYesv2.3 on cover page

Scoring Model

From judgement to a number teams can act on

Each dimension is scored on a defined 0–5 scale — 5 Excellent (fully meets best practice), 4 Good (minor gaps), 3 Adequate (usable but inconsistent), 2 Weak, 1 Poor, 0 N/A (not reviewable from the supplied content) — then normalised to 0.00–1.00 (raw ÷ 5, two decimals). N/A dimensions are excluded and their weight redistributed proportionally, with the exclusion stated openly — never guessed.

The overall score is a weighted sum using the official KQS weights, deliberately weighted towards the two dimensions that most affect answer quality:

DimensionWeightRationale
Clear30%Parseability decides what the LLM can extract from a chunk
Accurate30%Wrong content confidently retrieved is the worst failure mode
Relevant15%Content must answer real user questions, not describe abstractly
Time-bound15%Provenance and recency signals gate user trust
Retrievable10%Partly system-managed via the content register

The weighted result maps to a plain-language readiness verdict, with 0.80 as the effective "RAG-ready" threshold:

Overall KQSReadiness verdict
0.90 – 1.00Excellent — AI-ready; only minor or cosmetic edits
0.80 – 0.89Good — RAG-ready; improvements nice-to-have, not blocking
0.60 – 0.79Fair — usable with caution; improve before heavy reliance
0.40 – 0.59Weak — not suitable as a primary RAG source yet
0.00 – 0.39Poor — not suitable for RAG in current form

Transparency was a design requirement, not a nicety: every score ships with its derivation in plain business language — "the document defers critical role definitions to an external HR policy with no inline summary, so Clear = 3/5 → 0.60, down from 4 due to cross-reference dependency" — so a non-technical owner understands exactly why, and exactly what to change.

Mitigations

Never a penalty without a fix

Every cross-reference issue the evaluation flags is paired with one of five named mitigation strategies, matched to the severity and type of problem — each with copy-ready example edits the author can adapt directly:

mitigation_01

Inline summaries for critical dependencies

  • When a key term, rule or threshold is defined only in another document
  • Add a brief inline definition where the term is first used — "In brief: purchases under €5,000 may be approved by a line manager…" — so a lone chunk still answers usefully

mitigation_02

Embed context in every major section

  • When sections depend on context introduced elsewhere
  • Open each major section with a short "Context" or "Scope" paragraph restating key assumptions — RAG retrieves one section at a time

mitigation_03

Replace or annotate dead-end references

  • When the document says "see document X" with nothing inline
  • Embed the key rule alongside the reference — "Key rule: customer data must be deleted within 90 days of contract end…" — partial answers beat none

mitigation_04

Flag document clusters

  • When a document is one of several tightly interlinked files
  • List companion documents in the preamble and the register's referenced_documents field, so curators ingest the full cluster together

mitigation_05

Restructure hub-and-index documents

  • When a document is essentially an index with no standalone content
  • Merge it with its most-referenced companions, rewrite essential content inline, or explicitly mark it "not suitable as a standalone RAG source — ingest the companion documents instead"
  • Prevents the AI being asked to answer from a document that was never designed to contain answers

The evaluation itself lands as a structured, human-friendly report: a 3–5 bullet overview of strengths and RAG risks, the normalised score table against the 0.80 target, the provenance metadata check, an always-shown self-containedness flag (density and risk rated low / medium / high), dimension-by-dimension reasoning, and recommendations grouped Critical / High / Medium / Low — each with the issue, why it matters for RAG, what to do, example edits, and the expected KQS impact (e.g. +0.10 Time-bound, +0.03 Overall).
